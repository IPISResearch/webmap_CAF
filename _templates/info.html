Interactive webmap of the Central African Republic

Overview

The present map is an interactive webmap of the Central African Republic. It provides a visual representation of incidents, natural resources, armed actor areas of influence and other relevant dynamics relating to conflict and crisis in the Central African Republic.

How to use this map

In the legend on the right, you can select the datasets that you wish to render visible on the map. Within many layers, you can also filter for specific types of features using the filter options.

For many of the points, lines or polygons on the map, a pop-up window with additional information appears when clicking on them.

When clicking on incidents, this popup will appear in a popup pane to the right of the screen placing that incident in a timeline of events nationally. Note that many major or strategically important locations can contain multiple incident entries. These entries will appear in a chronological spiral with the most recent at the center, when the location is clicked on.

A histogram at the bottom of the page enables the user to get an overview of the severity of the conflict over time. This histogram also features a time slider, which allows to filter the incidents to only those within the selected period.

Given the difficulty of gathering precise geographic data and the poor quality of the information available, the maps inevitably contain inaccuracies and are not comprehensive. Therefore, all of the maps are to be considered indicative and limited, rather than an exact representation of the geographic features presented. A similar caveat applies to the summary statistics of the violent incidents presented in the legend of the map. The incidents shown cannot be considered to be exhaustive and are not necessarily suited for statistical analysis. IPIS welcomes any feedback or corrections at mapping@ipisresearch.be.

Sources

For the basemaps IPIS has made use of GIS data from OpenSteetMap, Mapbox, WRI, OCHA, the University of Bangui, the World Resources Institute (WRI), USAID and the Humanitarian and Development Partnership Team.

Additional sources used for specific layers are as listed here:
  • The incidents layer is based on reports from secondary sources, more specifically from ACLED, UN agencies, the LRA Crisis Tracker, local and international NGOs and media.
  • The natural resources layer is based on data from IPIS, the Central African Ministry of Mines, the Central African Ministry of Hydrocarbon, USGS, USAID, and the World Resources Institute.
  • The layer on cattle migration is based on research undertaken by an IPIS consultant, in consultation with experts from the FAO, FNEC and the University of Bangui.
  • The layer on armed groups shows approximate areas of control and influence in December 2017. It has been compiled with data from the UN Panel of Experts, UN agencies and the incident layer.
